Manuscripts in progress:

1) Fake News in India: A Field Experiment
This study tests the efficacy of a persuasive fact-checking intervention on ability to identify fake news during the 2019 elections in India, using a field experiment with 1200 respondents in Bihar. The study seeks to understand whether in-person fact-checking interventions increase ability to identify fake news, what kinds of misinformation are more resistant to corrections, and whether motivated reasoning affects partisans' responses.

2) How Does Political Polarization Affect Media Consumption?
The past decade has witnessed an explosion of interest in the partisan polarization of the American electorate. However scholarly investigation of this topic has conspicuously understudied polarization in developing democracies. In this study I test the effect that media source has on perceived credibility of information using two survey experiments and analyze whether partisan identity and polarization affect information consumption. Data comes from an orginal survey of 7,000 households across 4 states in India.

3) Why is Female Labor Force Participation So Low in India?
With Neelanjan Sircar
In this paper, we analyze preferences among women who are currently unemployed to enter the labor force through a survey experiment in the vicinity of the Indian cities of Patna and Varanasi. Our results are striking – women are 14 to 22 percentage points less likely to express a preference to take up a suitable job if they have to travel one hour to work. The magnitude of these effects are far greater than the impact of the primary wage earner of the household losing his/her job. This suggests that the impact of the lack of safe mobility for women on preferences to enter the labor market may be more severe than is commonly assumed, even more than income effects and lack of assistance at home – the major economic explanations for why women choose to leave the labor force.

3) The Effect of a Girl Child on Parental Attitudes and Behaviors
With Devesh Kapur
Sociologists and political scientists have demonstrated a link between offspring gender and parental beliefs and voting habits by demonstrating that parenting daughters increases liberal attitudes. We test this hypothesis in the context of urban India using a novel identification strategy that exploits the gender of one's first born child in India as random. We hypothesize that having a girl child first leads to a shift in respondent attitudes in the liberal direction. The paper utilizes original data from a representative survey of 5400 urban households in the National Capital Region of India.
