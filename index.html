1) "Educative Interventions to Combat Misinformation: Evidence From A Field Experiment in India."
Revise and resubmit. American Political Science Review.

Abstract: Misinformation makes democratic governance harder, especially in developing countries. Despite its real-world import, little is known about how to combat fake news outside of the U.S., particularly in places with low education, accelerating Internet access, and encrypted information sharing. This study uses a field experiment in India to test the efficacy of a pedagogical intervention on respondents' ability to identify fake news during the 2019 elections (N=1224). Treated respondents received in-person media literacy training in which enumerators demonstrated two tools to identify fake news: reverse image searching and navigating a fact-checking website. Receiving this hour-long media literacy intervention did not significantly increase respondents' ability to identify fake news on average. However, treated respondents who support the ruling party became significantly less able to identify pro-attitudinal fake news. These findings point to the resilience of misinformation in India and the presence of motivated reasoning in a traditionally non-ideological party system.

2) "I Don't Think That's True, Bro! An Experiment on Fact-checking Misinformation in India."
With Simon Chauchard and D.J. Flynn
br> Under review.

Abstract: Misinformation on encrypted messaging applications is linked to changes in public opinion, electoral outcomes, and even violence. Since encrypted platforms cannot control content, can social media users correct their peers' misperceptions? If so, how? We experimentally evaluate the effect of different types of corrective messages on the persistence of seven common rumors among a large sample of social media users in India (N=5104). We show that peer-to-peer corrections substantially reduce belief in misinformation. Neither motivated reasoning nor the sophistication of these corrective messages conditions their effects. Brief, unsourced and unsubstantiated corrections achieve an effect comparable to that of corrections backed by evidence from credible sources (domain experts and specialized fact-checkers alike). This suggests that merely signaling a doubt about a rumor (regardless of how substantiated this signal is) may go a long way in reducing misinformation. These results have implications for both users and platforms.

3) "Leveraging Group Identity Against Fake News: An Experiment on COVID-19 Misinformation in India"
With Simon Chauchard
Abstract: Misinformation related to COVID-19 is rampant. While false stories of miracle cures might lead people to resist evidence-based policies such as hand washing, rumors that scapegoat minorities have the potential to fuel communal hatred and polarization. These problems are more pronounced in countries with low digital literacy, reliance on traditional medicine, but increasing social polarization. How can we correct misinformed beliefs during crises, especially when they are rooted in tradition and social identities? In this study, we use an experiment to evaluate a targeted correction strategy to reduce COVID-19 misinformation in India (N=1250). We posit that belief in misinformation arises from pressures to conform to the ingroup and from dissonance between priors and new information. Our experiment tests whether corrections that appeal to ingroup identities can reduce this dissonance, thereby attenuating the uptake of misinformation in group settings. This work offers insights for researchers, policymakers and tech platforms on avenues to combat harmful COVID-19 and health misinformation in developing countries.

4) Does Political Polarization Affect Media Consumption? Experimental Evidence From India
The past decade has witnessed an explosion of interest in the partisan polarization of the American electorate. However scholarly investigation of this topic has conspicuously understudied polarization in developing democracies. In this study I test the effect that media source has on perceived credibility of information using two survey experiments and analyze whether partisan identity and polarization affect information consumption. Data comes from an orginal survey of 7,000 households across 4 states in India.

5) The Effect of a Girl Child on Parental Attitudes and Behaviors
With Devesh Kapur
Sociologists and political scientists have demonstrated a link between offspring gender and parental beliefs and voting habits by demonstrating that parenting daughters increases liberal attitudes. We test this hypothesis in the context of urban India using a novel identification strategy that exploits the gender of one's first born child in India as random. We hypothesize that having a girl child first leads to a shift in respondent attitudes in the liberal direction. The paper utilizes original data from a representative survey of 5400 urban households in the National Capital Region of India.
